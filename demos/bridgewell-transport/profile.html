🏢 About This Company

Curtis Rainey spent eleven years as a dispatcher for a regional LTL carrier in Springfield, Missouri before he scraped together enough credit to buy two used Freightliners in 2014 and start Bridgewell Transport out of a rented lot in Joplin. He picked Joplin on purpose — it sits inside a day's drive of Dallas, Little Rock, Kansas City, Tulsa, and Springfield, which let him build a dense regional network instead of chasing long-haul freight he couldn't service well with a small crew. The fleet grew steadily to ten trucks by 2019 and has held there since, while Curtis has focused on fixing what he calls "the leaks" before adding more equipment.

Bridgewell hauls exclusively dry van freight: pallets of lumber, roofing supplies, and drywall for building materials customers, and bagged seed, feed, and fertilizer for agricultural cooperatives across a four-state footprint (Missouri, Arkansas, Oklahoma, Kansas). Its four largest accounts — Ozark Building Supply (Joplin, MO), Route 66 Ag Cooperative (Tulsa, OK), Sooner State Lumber (Oklahoma City, OK), and Delta Farm & Feed (Little Rock, AR) — account for roughly 78% of freight volume, giving Curtis pricing leverage in theory but real cash-flow exposure in practice whenever any one of them has a slow month.

Curtis still dispatches himself most mornings out of a converted office next to the truck yard, working from a whiteboard and a handful of spreadsheets. He knows in his gut that certain trucks eat more fuel than they should, that one customer's dock crew is chronically slow, and that one of his lanes barely pays for itself — but until now he never had the data in front of him to prove it, size it, or decide what to do about it.

Bridgewell was selected as a demo target because its profile is common across small regional dry van fleets: real operating history (24 months of granular trip, fuel, and delivery data), a handful of specific, dollar-quantifiable problems hiding inside spreadsheets Curtis already keeps, and an owner-operator who is analytically minded but has never had tooling built for a fleet his size.

🔍 What We Found in the Data
The Joplin ↔ Little Rock lane nets only $1.46–$1.50/mile after fuel — the two worst-performing lanes in the network — against Curtis's own stated $2.05/mile breakeven target. Combined, the lane carries 791 loads and ~$470K in revenue, but its true margin trails every other lane, including the best lane (Joplin ↔ Springfield) at $2.55–$2.61/mile.
TRK-006 burns ~$1,287/month in excess fuel. This 2013 Freightliner Cascadia with 738,000+ miles averages 5.20 MPG versus a 6.95 MPG average across the rest of the fleet — an estimated 7,505 extra gallons consumed over the 24-month data window.
TRK-006 also leads the fleet in maintenance cost ($42,912) and downtime (461 hours) — more than 4x the next-highest truck — and it's disproportionately assigned to the Little Rock lane, compounding that lane's already-thin economics.
Ozark Building Supply averages 47.7 minutes of detention per delivery versus a 10.5-minute average everywhere else, across 476 stops — roughly 295 excess driver-hours (about 12/month) spent waiting at the dock instead of driving.
Ozark's on-time rate is just 3.4% compared to 85–92% at every other customer, even though trucks generally arrive on schedule — the dock wait, not the drive, is the failure point.
Top 4 customers represent 78.4% of revenue, and Route 66 Ag Cooperative (16.4% of revenue, ~$9,553/month) pays on a de facto net-60 cycle against net-30 terms — tying up roughly a full month of revenue in receivables beyond contract terms.
3 of 14 drivers (21%) have been terminated, and all three were hired within an 18-month window (Jan–Jul 2025) — meaning 30% of drivers hired since January 2025 didn't make it, versus zero separations among the other ten drivers.

⚠️ Primary Pain Points Identified

Analysis of the data revealed the following operational pain points, ranked by estimated annual dollar impact.

1 The Joplin ↔ Little Rock Lane Is Structurally Unprofitable
Both directions of this lane (ROUTE-01, ROUTE-02) net only $1.46–$1.50/mile after fuel — the two worst lanes in Bridgewell's network — against Curtis's own $2.05/mile breakeven target. It carries 791 loads and roughly $470,000 in revenue, but true margin is far thinner than any other lane (the best lane, Joplin ↔ Springfield, nets $2.55–$2.61/mile). Bridgewell has kept the lane running largely out of loyalty to Delta Farm & Feed, without ever pricing out what that loyalty is costing.
2 TRK-006 Is a Rolling Cost Center
This 2013 Freightliner Cascadia with 738,000+ miles averages 5.20 MPG versus a 6.95 MPG average across the rest of the fleet, burning an estimated 7,505 extra gallons over the 24-month data window — about $1,287/month in excess fuel cost alone. It also leads the fleet in total maintenance spend ($42,912, more than 4x the next truck) and downtime (461 hours), driven by repeated engine, transmission, and coolant-system repairs consistent with its age and mileage, and it's disproportionately assigned to the Little Rock lane that's already the least profitable in the network.
3 Ozark Building Supply's Dock Is Destroying On-Time Performance
Ozark Building Supply averages 47.7 minutes of detention per delivery versus a 10.5-minute average at every other customer — a 37-minute gap repeated across 476 stops, or roughly 295 excess driver-hours (about 12 hours/month). Ozark's on-time rate sits at a startling 3.4% versus 85–92% elsewhere, meaning virtually every delivery there is scored late even though the truck usually arrives on schedule — the dock wait, not the drive, is the failure point, and it's driven by a chronically understaffed receiving dock.
4 Customer Concentration and Slow Pay Create Cash-Flow Risk
Bridgewell's top 4 customers account for 78.4% of revenue, giving Curtis pricing leverage in theory but real exposure in practice. Route 66 Ag Cooperative (16.4% of revenue, ~$9,553/month) pays on a de facto net-60 cycle despite net-30 contract terms, meaning roughly a full month of revenue — about $9,500 — is perpetually tied up in receivables beyond terms, a working-capital drag that never shows up until cash gets tight.
5 Driver Turnover Is Concentrated in Recent Hires
3 of Bridgewell's 14 drivers (21%) have been terminated, and all three were hired within an 18-month window between January and July 2025 — meaning 30% of drivers hired since January 2025 didn't last, versus zero separations among the other ten drivers. Notably, drivers primarily paired with the unprofitable Little Rock lane and worn-out TRK-006 make up a disproportionate share of that group, suggesting the lane and the truck are a retention problem as much as a cost problem, and each early departure erodes onboarding investment and forces repeated hiring costs.
