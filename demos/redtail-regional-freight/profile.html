🏢 About This Company

Dale Whitfield spent eleven years driving oilfield support trucks out of Midland before he bought his first truck in 2009, right as the shale boom was reshaping freight demand across the Panhandle. He started Redtail Regional Freight with a single 2006 Peterbilt and a handshake contract hauling frac sand and ag inputs between Amarillo and Lubbock. By the early 2010s he'd added reefer capacity to chase food-processing freight, and the company settled into its current identity: a dry van/reefer hybrid regional carrier running a tight corridor across the Texas Panhandle, eastern New Mexico, western Oklahoma, and southern Colorado.

Today Redtail runs 38 trucks and employs 52 drivers out of a single terminal in Amarillo. The fleet is a mix of newer Freightliner Cascadias and Peterbilts bought in the last five years and a stubborn tail of older trucks Dale has been reluctant to retire because they're paid off. The company's bread and butter is agricultural and food freight — beef, dairy, produce, and packaged foods moving out of Panhandle processing plants to distribution centers in Denver, Oklahoma City, and Albuquerque, plus backhauls of packaging materials and dry goods. Four anchor customers — Panhandle Beef Processors, Llano Estacado Foods, High Plains Ag Cooperative, and Sangre de Cristo Distribution — together with two smaller accounts account for the large majority of Redtail's freight volume.

Dale still works the dispatch desk most mornings and prides himself on knowing every driver by name. But the operation has outgrown his spreadsheet-and-gut-feel management style. He knows something is dragging down margins on the Denver reefer lane and suspects one of his older trucks is a money pit, but he's never had the data laid out in front of him to prove it or size the problem. That combination — a real, profitable business run entirely on instinct, sitting on two years of transaction-level data nobody has ever queried — is exactly the kind of situation this analysis was built to surface.

⚠️ Primary Pain Points Identified

Analysis of the data revealed the following operational pain points, ranked by estimated annual dollar impact.

1 TRK-014 Fuel Inefficiency
TRK-014, a 2013 Freightliner Cascadia with 781,400 miles, averages 5.04 MPG across 528 trips versus a 6.90 MPG fleet average — a 27% efficiency gap that shows up on nearly every single trip (std dev of just 0.21), not as an occasional bad tank. At current diesel prices (~$4.20/gal fleet average), this one truck burns roughly 35% more fuel per mile than the fleet norm, and it's been that way long enough that it's fully baked into the truck's cost profile rather than a fixable mechanical fluke.
2 TRK-014 Maintenance & Downtime Concentration
The same truck has racked up $123,170 in total lifetime maintenance cost — over 11x the fleet average of $10,765 per truck — with $89,439 of that (73%) incurred in just the last 18 months across 17 separate service events. Downtime tells the same story: 1,097.9 lifetime hours, 634.5 of them (58%) in the trailing 18 months alone, which works out to roughly 26 lost operating days from a single truck in a year and a half.
3 Llano Estacado Foods Detention Outlier
Llano Estacado Foods (CUST-002), Redtail's dairy and packaged-foods customer in Lubbock, generates average detention of 46.6 minutes per delivery across 8,950 events — a 4.4x gap over the 10.6-minute average every other customer produces, and it holds consistently rather than showing up as an occasional spike. That's 6,946 cumulative hours of driver and truck time tied up at one dock, effectively unpaid idle time that displaces revenue-generating miles and is currently invisible, buried inside "normal" trip variance.
4 Reefer & Backhaul Lanes Losing Money After Fuel
Once fuel is netted against revenue and fuel surcharge, the Amarillo→Denver reefer lane (AMA-DEN-REEFER) nets just $1.94/mile, the Denver→Amarillo backhaul nets $1.74/mile, and the Amarillo→Oklahoma City backhaul nets only $1.69/mile — all below the $2.05/mile reference breakeven despite posted base rates of $1.90–$2.15/mile that look reasonable at a glance. On the reefer lane specifically, 79.6% of individual loads fall below breakeven; on both backhaul lanes, essentially every load (99.5%+) does, meaning Dale has been running a substantial share of his network at a structural loss without a spreadsheet ever catching it.
5 New-Hire Driver Attrition
Of the 12 drivers hired at the Amarillo terminal in the last 18 months, 7 (58.3%) have already been terminated or quit, compared to a 5.0% termination rate among drivers hired earlier — a churn rate over 11x higher for new hires. This looks like a structural onboarding or early-route-assignment problem concentrated at the front end of the employment lifecycle, driving recruiting and training costs up while never giving new drivers the chance to become productive.
