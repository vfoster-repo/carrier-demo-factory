🏢 About This Company

Walter Braddock spent fourteen years as a dispatcher for Union Pacific's Hillyard rail yard in Spokane before the yard's regional consolidation in 2002 sent his job to Omaha. Rather than relocate, Walter took his severance, bought two used Freightliners, and started hauling freight for the paper mills and warehouses that had relied on the rail yard he'd just lost. He named the company Cascade Summit Freightways after the mountain pass his first loads ran through on the way to Seattle.

Twenty-three years later, Cascade Summit has grown into a 110-truck, 165-driver dry van carrier with a genuine national footprint, still headquartered at its original Spokane terminal but now running a satellite terminal in Chicago to anchor its Midwest freight. The company's bread-and-butter lanes connect the Pacific Northwest to the industrial Midwest and the Sun Belt: Spokane to Chicago, Spokane to Dallas, Portland to Denver, and Seattle to Atlanta. Walter, now 61, still comes into the Spokane terminal most mornings, though his daughter Renee has taken over daily dispatch operations. Four anchor accounts — Cascade Paper Products, Evergreen Foods Co-op, TitanBolt Industrial Supply, and Northwind Appliance Distributors — account for the bulk of the company's volume.

Cascade Summit runs a mixed fleet that spans a decade of Freightliner and Peterbilt purchases, with newer trucks concentrated on the long Sun Belt lanes and older equipment cycling through shorter regional work. The Chicago satellite terminal, opened in 2022 to reduce deadhead on the Spokane-Chicago-Dallas triangle, was staffed with a wave of new driver hires in 2023 — a decision that solved one problem and, as the data shows, quietly created another.

Cascade Summit is a classic mid-size carrier success story that has outgrown its founder's gut-feel management style. Walter and Renee both sense that "something's off" with a few accounts and a few trucks, but nobody had sat down and quantified it — dispatch decisions were still being made lane-by-lane and load-by-load without a clear view of which combinations of truck, customer, and lane were actually profitable. That gap between healthy-looking aggregate numbers and real, dollar-quantified problems hiding underneath is exactly what made this company a compelling analysis target.

⚠️ Primary Pain Points Identified

Analysis of the data revealed the following operational pain points, ranked by estimated annual dollar impact.

1 The Seattle↔Atlanta Lane Is Structurally Unprofitable
The Seattle↔Atlanta lane pair (RTE-04/RTE-08) nets only $1.30-$1.39/mile after fuel costs — well below the company's $2.05/mile breakeven target — despite running 11,656 loads and roughly 31 million miles over the data window at full, normal volume (~480 loads/month). The shortfall versus breakeven annualizes to an estimated $10.7M, the single largest quantified pain point in the dataset, yet dispatch has continued scheduling it as if it were a healthy lane because lane performance has never been evaluated as its own line item.
2 An Aging 8-Truck Cohort Is Quietly Draining Maintenance Budget
The TRK-041 through TRK-048 cohort — eight 2010-2012 model-year trucks — averages $101,488 in maintenance cost and 1,015 downtime hours per truck per year, versus a fleet average of just $18,469 and 156 hours: 5.5x the cost and 6.5x the downtime concentrated in 8 of 110 trucks. This same cohort clusters at 5.9-5.96 MPG, well below every other truck in the fleet, compounding fuel waste on top of the maintenance drag and costing an estimated $354,000/year in excess maintenance spend alone.
3 One Worn-Out Truck Is Burning Six Figures a Year in Fuel
Truck TRK-047, a 2011 Peterbilt 389 with roughly 880,000 miles, averages just 4.92 MPG against a fleet average of 6.78 MPG — a 27% efficiency gap sustained across 606 trips and ~1.15 million miles. That gap costs an estimated $10,972 per month, or roughly $263,000 over the 24-month data window, in excess fuel versus what a fleet-average truck covering the same miles would burn — and it is the worst outlier within the broader aging cohort described above.
4 TitanBolt Industrial Supply's Dock Is Taxing Every Load
TitanBolt Industrial Supply (Dallas, TX), Cascade Summit's #3 account by revenue at $38.3M (14.6% of total), averages 47.4 minutes of detention per stop versus 10.6 minutes for every other customer combined — a 4.4x gap — and only 2.4% of TitanBolt stops arrive on time, compared to 80-90%+ for every other account. At a conservative $75/hour opportunity-cost rate, this is costing an estimated $15,900/month in idle driver and truck time, on an account too large to simply walk away from.
5 Chicago's 2023 Hiring Class Is Churning at 4x the Normal Rate
The Chicago satellite terminal's 2023 driver hiring cohort — 21 drivers — has a 61.9% termination rate, versus 14.0% for the Spokane terminal overall and 20.0% fleet-wide. Spokane's own 2023 cohort, hired in the same calendar year under the same market conditions, lost only 25% of its drivers — pointing to an onboarding, pay, or home-time problem specific to how the Chicago terminal was staffed and run in 2023, rather than a generic driver-market churn story.
