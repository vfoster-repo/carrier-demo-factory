# Cascade Summit Freightways

**Type:** mid_size_carrier
**Location:** Spokane, Washington
**Founded:** 2003
**Owner:** Walter Braddock
**Primary Freight:** Dry van
**Fleet Size:** 110 trucks, 165 drivers
**Tagline:** A Pacific Northwest dry van carrier that grew from two used trucks into a national 110-truck operation, still hauling freight the way its railroad-dispatcher founder learned to move it — reliably, or not at all.

## Company Story

Walter Braddock spent fourteen years as a dispatcher for Union Pacific's Hillyard rail yard in Spokane before the yard's regional consolidation in 2002 sent his job to Omaha. Rather than relocate, Walter took his severance, bought two used Freightliners, and started hauling freight for the paper mills and warehouses that had relied on the rail yard he'd just lost. He named the company Cascade Summit Freightways after the mountain pass his first loads ran through on the way to Seattle.

Twenty-three years later, Cascade Summit has grown into a 110-truck, 165-driver dry van carrier with a genuine national footprint, still headquartered at its original Spokane terminal but now running a satellite terminal in Chicago to anchor its Midwest freight. The company's bread-and-butter lanes connect the Pacific Northwest to the industrial Midwest and the Sun Belt: Spokane to Chicago, Spokane to Dallas, Portland to Denver, and Seattle to Atlanta. Walter, now 61, still comes into the Spokane terminal most mornings, though his daughter Renee has taken over daily dispatch operations.

Cascade Summit's customer base reflects its Pacific Northwest roots and its Midwest expansion. Cascade Paper Products, a Spokane-based mill, ships palletized paper stock on a near-daily cadence. Evergreen Foods Co-op out of Portland moves shelf-stable grocery freight to distribution centers nationwide. TitanBolt Industrial Supply, a Dallas-based fastener and hardware distributor, is Cascade Summit's largest single account by volume — and, as Renee will tell you, its most frustrating dock to deal with. Northwind Appliance Distributors in Chicago rounds out the four anchor accounts, moving major appliances that justify the backhaul capacity on the Spokane-Chicago lane.

Day to day, Cascade Summit runs a mixed fleet that spans a decade of Freightliner and Peterbilt purchases, with newer trucks concentrated on the long Sun Belt lanes and older equipment cycling through shorter regional work. The company opened its Chicago satellite terminal in 2022 to reduce deadhead on the Spokane-Chicago-Dallas triangle, staffing it with a wave of new driver hires — a decision that solved one problem and, as the data shows, created another.

## Key Operational Facts

- Fleet: 110 trucks (TRK-001 through TRK-110), primarily Freightliner Cascadia and Peterbilt 579/389 models, years 2010-2024.
- Drivers: 165 total (DRV-001 through DRV-165), home-based at either the Spokane terminal or the Chicago satellite terminal (opened 2022).
- Primary customers:
  - Cascade Paper Products (Spokane, WA) — paper/pulp industry
  - Evergreen Foods Co-op (Portland, OR) — food/grocery industry
  - TitanBolt Industrial Supply (Dallas, TX) — industrial/hardware distribution
  - Northwind Appliance Distributors (Chicago, IL) — appliance distribution
- Primary lanes: Spokane WA↔Chicago IL, Spokane WA↔Dallas TX, Portland OR↔Denver CO, Seattle WA↔Atlanta GA (plus a handful of secondary regional lanes for route variety).
- Truck TRK-047 is a 2011 Freightliner Cascadia with roughly 880,000 miles on it, the oldest high-mileage unit still in active rotation.
- A cohort of 8 trucks (2010-2012 model years: TRK-041 through TRK-048) represents the aging tail of the fleet and drives a disproportionate share of maintenance cost and downtime.
- The Chicago satellite terminal, opened in 2022, has a driver cohort (~20 drivers hired in 2023) with markedly elevated termination/turnover rates.

## Pain Points (to be discovered analytically)

- **Truck TRK-047**, a 2011 Freightliner Cascadia with ~880,000 miles, averages **4.9 MPG** on its runs versus a fleet average of roughly **6.9 MPG** — a 29% efficiency gap that has persisted across hundreds of trips.
- **TitanBolt Industrial Supply** (Dallas, TX), Cascade Summit's largest account by volume, averages **~48 minutes of detention per delivery**, more than 4x the fleet average of **~11 minutes** — and this account is too large to simply drop.
- The **Seattle→Atlanta lane** nets roughly **$1.55/mile** after fuel costs, well below the company's **$2.05/mile** breakeven threshold, for the large majority of loads run on it — yet the lane continues to be scheduled at normal volume.
- The **Chicago satellite terminal's 2023 driver hiring cohort** (~20 drivers) shows a termination/turnover rate several times higher than the Spokane-based driver population, suggesting an onboarding, pay, or home-time problem specific to that terminal.
- The **aging truck cohort (TRK-041 through TRK-048, model years 2010-2012)** generates maintenance costs and downtime hours far above the fleet average, quietly eating into the margins on whatever lanes they're assigned to.

## What This Company Needs

Cascade Summit is a classic mid-size carrier success story that has outgrown its founder's gut-feel management style. Walter and Renee both sense that "something's off" with a few accounts and a few trucks, but nobody has sat down and quantified it — dispatch decisions are still made lane-by-lane and load-by-load without a clear view of which combinations of truck, customer, and lane are actually profitable. What would genuinely help here is a straightforward profitability and efficiency lens: a way to rank trucks by true operating cost (fuel + maintenance + downtime) rather than just fuel, a way to flag customers whose detention time is silently taxing margin on every load, and a way to see which lanes are structurally unprofitable versus just having a bad month. On the people side, a driver retention view that isolates the Chicago cohort from the rest of the fleet would let Renee finally address whether the Chicago terminal itself is the problem, rather than assuming it's "just some bad hires."
